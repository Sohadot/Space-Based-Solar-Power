The Caltech Space Solar Power Project is an academic research programme that achieved a significant milestone in early 2023 with the MAPLE (Microwave Array for Power-transfer Low-orbit Experiment) experiment, which demonstrated wireless power transmission from orbit for the first time in a controlled experiment aboard the Caltech SSPD-1 satellite. The MAPLE experiment transmitted power wirelessly in space and directed a small amount of power toward Earth, representing a proof-of-concept demonstration of orbital wireless power beaming. The Caltech SSPP continues active research on lightweight photovoltaic and microwave transmission architectures. The experiment was a research demonstration, not a commercial or operational power delivery capability.

Institutional Context

The Caltech Space Solar Power Project is funded through a major philanthropic gift and operates as an academic research programme within Caltech. The project team spans electrical engineering, aerospace, and materials science disciplines. The SSPD-1 satellite was a technology demonstration satellite launched in early 2023 that carried three experimental payloads, including MAPLE. The project's academic outputs contribute to the global SBSP knowledge base and have received significant international attention.

SBSP Relevance

The Caltech SSPP is directly relevant as the source of the first confirmed orbital wireless power transmission demonstration experiment. The MAPLE experiment established a proof-of-concept milestone for the orbital power beaming subsystem of SBSP, advancing TRL for this key capability. Caltech's lightweight photovoltaic and microwave tile architecture research is also relevant to the mass-to-orbit challenge central to SBSP feasibility.

Technology Relationship

Caltech SSPP research engages directly with phased array transmitter systems, high-efficiency solar cells, modular spacecraft architecture, power conversion and conditioning, and microwave power transmission. The MAPLE experiment demonstrated the beam steering and wireless power transmission elements of the SBSP transmission subsystem.

Feasibility Boundary

The MAPLE experiment demonstrated wireless power transmission from orbit at milliwatt scale in a controlled research experiment. This demonstration does not establish commercial, operational, or utility-scale wireless power delivery capability. The gap between milliwatt-scale research demonstration and gigawatt-scale operational SBSP remains the defining feasibility boundary.

Claim Boundary

The MAPLE experiment should be described as a proof-of-concept research demonstration of orbital wireless power beaming, not as a demonstration of SBSP commercial viability or operational readiness. Caltech SSPP research advances component and subsystem TRL, not system-level operational capability.
