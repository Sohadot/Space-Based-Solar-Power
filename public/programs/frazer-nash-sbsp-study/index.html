National Research Program

The Frazer-Nash Consultancy feasibility study, commissioned by the UK government, produced one of the most comprehensive and publicly available national SBSP feasibility assessments published in the 2020s. The study examined system architectures, technology readiness levels, cost trajectories, environmental impacts, regulatory considerations, and policy frameworks for a potential UK SBSP programme. It concluded that SBSP was technically feasible in principle but required substantial technology development and cost reduction before economic viability could be established. The study is a primary reference document for UK and international SBSP policy discourse.

Institutional Context

The Frazer-Nash study was commissioned by the UK government as an independent technical and economic assessment to inform UK space and energy policy. Frazer-Nash Consultancy engaged technical experts, industry stakeholders, and academic contributors to produce a comprehensive assessment that drew on international SBSP research literature and conducted original analysis of UK-specific conditions.

SBSP Relevance

The Frazer-Nash study is one of the most widely cited national feasibility assessments in the global SBSP discourse. Its systematic methodology, transparent assumptions, and comprehensive coverage of technical, economic, and policy dimensions make it a reference standard for national SBSP feasibility assessment. The study's conclusions on technology readiness and cost trajectories are directly relevant to the claim boundary framework of this reference layer.

Technology Relationship

The Frazer-Nash study assessed all major SBSP technology subsystems, including high-efficiency solar cells, microwave power transmission, phased array transmitters, in-space assembly, modular spacecraft architecture, and ground receiving infrastructure, with TRL assessments for each subsystem.

Feasibility Boundary

The Frazer-Nash study concluded that SBSP is technically feasible in principle at TRL levels of individual subsystems, but that system-level integration and demonstration at scale has not been achieved. Economic feasibility requires cost reductions not yet demonstrated.

Claim Boundary

The Frazer-Nash study should be cited as a feasibility assessment, not as confirmation that SBSP is economically viable or ready for development commitment. Its conclusions identify substantial remaining technology and cost challenges and should not be selectively cited to suggest readiness beyond what the study itself concludes.
