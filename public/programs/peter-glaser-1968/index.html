Historical Foundational

Peter Glaser's 1968 proposal, published in Science magazine, is the foundational concept document for space-based solar power. Glaser proposed collecting solar energy in geostationary orbit using large photovoltaic arrays and transmitting the energy to Earth via microwave wireless power transmission to ground-based receiving antennas. The proposal identified the core architectural elements — orbital solar collection, wireless power transmission, and ground reception — that define all subsequent SBSP concepts. Glaser's 1968 paper is the origin point of the SBSP field and is universally cited as the foundational reference in international SBSP research literature. The proposal was conceptual and no technology existed at the time to implement it.

Institutional Context

Peter Glaser was a scientist at Arthur D. Little and later associated with the Smithsonian Institution. His 1968 Science paper 'Power from the Sun: Its Future' introduced the satellite solar power station concept and stimulated subsequent US government interest that led to the 1979 NASA reference system study. Glaser's proposal emerged from 1960s thinking about long-range energy solutions and was informed by early space programme achievements that made geostationary orbit a practical operational environment.

SBSP Relevance

Glaser's 1968 proposal is the essential historical origin of the SBSP field. All subsequent SBSP architectural concepts, feasibility studies, and national programmes trace their intellectual lineage to Glaser's foundational concept. Understanding Glaser's original proposal is necessary for contextualising the subsequent history of SBSP research, including the reasons why the concept attracted NASA study in the 1970s and why it was not pursued further at that time.

Technology Relationship

Glaser's 1968 proposal conceptually identified the three primary technology subsystems of SBSP: solar collection (photovoltaics), wireless power transmission (microwave beaming), and ground reception (rectenna). None of these subsystems existed at operational scale in 1968, and Glaser's proposal was a conceptual forward projection, not an engineering design based on demonstrated technology.

Feasibility Boundary

Glaser's 1968 proposal was a conceptual proposal, not an engineering design or feasibility study. The technologies required for SBSP did not exist at operational scale in 1968 and remain below operational readiness today. The proposal's significance is conceptual and historical, not as evidence of technical feasibility at the time of publication.

Claim Boundary

Glaser's 1968 proposal should be cited as the foundational historical concept for SBSP, not as evidence that SBSP was technically feasible in 1968 or that subsequent progress has resolved the challenges Glaser identified. The proposal identified the concept; it did not validate the technology or the economics.
