The Indian Space Research Organisation has engaged with space-based solar power as a long-term energy technology concept relevant to India's energy security and space programme objectives. ISRO study activities have examined SBSP system architectures, technology readiness gaps, and the potential role of India's expanding launch and satellite capabilities in a future SBSP programme. India's growing domestic energy demand and space sector maturity position ISRO as an increasingly relevant national institution in the global SBSP research landscape, though no funded SBSP development programme has been confirmed.

Institutional Context

ISRO's engagement with SBSP occurs within the context of India's dual mandate of advancing its space programme and addressing domestic energy security. India's large renewable energy ambitions and growing space launch capabilities, including the development of heavy lift vehicles, create a national context in which SBSP warrants institutional attention. ISRO study activities interface with Indian academic institutions and the national energy policy framework.

SBSP Relevance

ISRO's SBSP study activities represent an important data point in the global spread of space agency engagement with SBSP. India's institutional attention to SBSP reflects the technology's emergence as a serious policy consideration among major spacefaring nations, not merely the original proponent states of the US, Europe, and Japan.

Technology Relationship

ISRO SBSP studies engage with high-efficiency solar cells, heavy lift launch requirements, and wireless power transmission as primary technology domains. India's developing expertise in satellite technology and launch systems is relevant to future SBSP system component development.

Feasibility Boundary

ISRO's SBSP activities are study-phase engagements, not funded development programmes. No ISRO SBSP development programme of record has been publicly confirmed. All assessments reflect early-stage institutional interest.

Claim Boundary

ISRO's SBSP study activities do not represent a commitment to develop or deploy a space-based solar power system. Institutional engagement at study phase should not be interpreted as a development decision or near-term deployment trajectory.
