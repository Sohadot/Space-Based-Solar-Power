Microwave Power Transmission from Orbit

Technology Reference

Microwave power transmission (MPT) is the primary wireless energy transfer modality studied for space-based solar power, using microwave-frequency electromagnetic radiation—typically at 2.45 GHz or 5.8 GHz—to beam collected solar energy from a geostationary satellite to a ground receiving station. The satellite converts DC electrical power from photovoltaic arrays into microwave radiation via magnetrons or solid-state amplifiers, directs the beam using a large phased-array antenna toward a ground rectenna array, and the rectenna converts the received microwave energy back into DC electricity. Microwave frequencies are chosen for their relatively low atmospheric attenuation, ability to penetrate cloud cover, and mature amplifier technology. At the power densities proposed for SBSP (≤23 mW/cm² at beam center for safety standards), microwave power transmission is considered safe for wildlife and human exposure in the rectenna exclusion zone. Ground demonstration of microwave power transmission over distances of tens to hundreds of meters has been performed by multiple research groups.

Infrastructure Role

Microwave power transmission is the core function of the SBSP transmission subsystem. It bridges the gap between orbital solar collection and ground-based power delivery, enabling the fundamental energy transfer that makes SBSP a power generation concept rather than simply an orbital platform. Transmission efficiency and beam control accuracy are primary determinants of system-level energy conversion efficiency.

Subsystem Relationship

Microwave transmission is enabled by the phased-array transmitter subsystem on the satellite, which must maintain phase coherence across thousands of antenna elements. It delivers power to the rectenna receiving array on the ground. Atmospheric attenuation and weather effects influence received power levels. Safety and regulatory systems must monitor beam pointing accuracy and enforce exclusion zones.

Feasibility and Readiness

Microwave power transmission at component level is at TRL 9 for terrestrial and radar applications. Long-distance (>36,000 km) space-to-ground MPT at SBSP power levels has not been demonstrated. End-to-end efficiency for a geostationary SBSP link at proposed power levels remains modeled rather than empirically verified at scale. Phased-array systems at the antenna apertures required for SBSP represent a significant engineering challenge.

Feasibility and Claim Boundary

This page documents microwave power transmission as studied and demonstrated for SBSP applications based on published technical literature and program reports. It does not claim that geostationary-to-ground MPT at gigawatt power levels has been demonstrated or that the safety of such a system at full scale is fully characterized. Safety standards cited reflect existing regulatory guidance, not operational SBSP system validation.
