In-Space Assembly Methods for SBSP Structures

Technology Reference

In-space assembly methods for space-based solar power satellites encompass the techniques, robotics, and operational procedures required to construct large orbital structures—potentially several kilometers in dimension—from components delivered to geostationary orbit. Unlike conventional spacecraft assembled on the ground before launch, SBSP satellites at gigawatt scale far exceed the payload capacity of any launch vehicle and must be built in orbit from multiple smaller delivered modules. Assembly approaches under study include teleoperated robotic assembly using specialized construction robots or orbital construction vehicles, autonomous structural assembly guided by machine vision and precise positioning systems, and hybrid approaches combining automated module docking with robotic connection and verification. Key challenges include precision alignment of large lightweight structures in microgravity, management of residual orbital dynamics during assembly, maintaining structural integrity of partially assembled configurations, and conducting assembly operations efficiently enough to be economically viable given the high cost of operational time at GEO.

Infrastructure Role

In-space assembly is an enabling technology for the orbital construction subsystem of SBSP. Without viable in-space assembly methods, SBSP at gigawatt scale is fundamentally limited by launch vehicle fairing dimensions and payload capacity. Assembly capability determines whether modular SBSP architectures can be practically built and what the construction time and cost will be for the orbital platform.

Subsystem Relationship

In-space assembly interfaces with the launch and logistics subsystem that delivers structural modules, with the robotic operations and servicing subsystem that performs physical assembly, and with the structural design of the satellite modules themselves (which must be designed for robotic handling and connection). Propulsion systems provide station-keeping during assembly. Power systems must supply energy to construction robots and assembly operations.

Feasibility and Readiness

Basic in-space assembly has been demonstrated through the construction of the International Space Station (TRL 9 for human-assisted assembly of large structures). Autonomous robotic assembly of the scale and precision required for SBSP (km-scale structures, potentially without crew) is at TRL 3–5. No orbital demonstration of robotic assembly of SBSP-representative structural modules exists.

Feasibility and Claim Boundary

This page describes in-space assembly methods for SBSP based on published technical studies and the history of large structure assembly in orbit. It does not claim that automated robotic assembly of SBSP-scale structures has been demonstrated or that the cost and time estimates for such assembly in conceptual studies have been validated. ISS assembly precedent involved extensive human crew activity not currently scalable to SBSP construction timelines.
