Heat Pipe and Radiator Systems for SBSP

Technology Reference

Heat pipe and radiator systems are the primary passive thermal transport and rejection mechanisms for spacecraft, and they play a central role in the thermal management architecture of space-based solar power satellites. A heat pipe is a closed, sealed tube or flat channel containing a working fluid that transfers heat from a hot evaporator section to a cooler condenser section by evaporation and condensation, driven by capillary wicking action without requiring pumps or active components. Heat pipes can transport heat over substantial distances with very low thermal resistance, making them effective for spreading heat from compact high-power sources to large radiating surfaces. Spacecraft radiators are panels with high emissivity coatings that dissipate heat by thermal radiation to space at temperatures determined by the Stefan-Boltzmann relationship. For SBSP, the radiator area required to reject waste heat from a gigawatt-class system is very large, and the mass of radiator panels and associated heat pipe networks contributes substantially to the satellite's total mass budget. Lightweight carbon-fiber composite radiators and advanced heat pipe materials (carbon nanotube wicks, lithium heat pipes for high-temperature operation) are research areas relevant to reducing this mass.

Infrastructure Role

Heat pipe and radiator systems are the primary heat rejection infrastructure of the SBSP satellite thermal management subsystem. They determine the maximum waste heat that can be rejected from the satellite, set the operating temperature limits of power electronics and solar cells, and contribute significantly to satellite mass. The radiator design is a major factor in the feasibility of achieving the specific mass (kg/kW) targets needed for economic SBSP.

Subsystem Relationship

Heat pipes connect high-heat-density components (power amplifiers, power conditioning circuits) to radiator panels distributed across the satellite structure. Radiator orientation relative to the sun affects radiating temperature and efficiency—radiators must be positioned to maximize view to cold space while avoiding direct solar illumination. Structural design must accommodate the mass and geometric requirements of the radiator system.

Feasibility and Readiness

Heat pipes and spacecraft radiators are at TRL 9 for conventional satellites. Lightweight advanced heat pipe systems and composite radiators are at TRL 5–7 in development programs. Designing heat pipe and radiator systems for SBSP-scale continuous waste heat rejection at the specific mass targets required has not been demonstrated. The thermal management mass budget is one of the most challenging aspects of meeting SBSP specific mass goals.

Feasibility and Claim Boundary

This page documents heat pipe and radiator technology for SBSP applications based on established spacecraft thermal engineering and published SBSP studies. It does not claim that heat pipe or radiator solutions have been designed or tested specifically for SBSP-scale systems, or that mass targets for SBSP thermal systems are achievable with current technology. Advanced thermal management concepts cited in research programs are at early development stages.
