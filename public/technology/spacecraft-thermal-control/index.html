Spacecraft Thermal Control for SBSP Satellites

Technology Reference

Spacecraft thermal control for space-based solar power satellites manages the thermal environment of the orbital platform to maintain all components—solar cells, power electronics, structural elements, transmitter modules—within their operating temperature ranges throughout the satellite's operational lifetime. The SBSP thermal environment presents particular challenges: the satellite operates in continuous or near-continuous sunlight in GEO, which means it cannot rely on eclipse cooling as some satellite designs do; large solar arrays intercept and absorb substantial solar power as heat in addition to converting it to electricity; and power electronics operating at high power densities generate significant waste heat that must be rejected to space via thermal radiation. In the vacuum of space, thermal radiation is the only available heat rejection mechanism (conduction and convection are absent). Thermal control systems on SBSP satellites will include passive radiation control through surface coatings and geometric design, heat spreading systems to distribute thermal loads, heat pipe networks to transport heat to radiator panels, deployable or body-mounted radiators, and potentially active thermal control loops for high-heat-density components.

Infrastructure Role

Thermal control is a critical enabling subsystem for SBSP satellites—without effective thermal management, photovoltaic cells operate at elevated temperatures that reduce conversion efficiency, power electronics exceed their thermal operating limits, and structural components experience differential thermal expansion that can damage the assembly. The mass of thermal management systems (radiators, heat pipes) is a significant component of the satellite's total mass budget.

Subsystem Relationship

Thermal management interfaces with every heat-generating subsystem: solar cells, power electronics, transmitter elements, and propulsion systems. Radiator area requirements are determined by the total waste heat load and the available radiator temperature differential between the hot side and the 4 K space environment. Structural design must integrate thermal radiator panels and heat pipe routing. Solar array geometry affects the thermal environment of adjacent components.

Feasibility and Readiness

Spacecraft thermal control technology is at TRL 9 for conventional satellites. The specific challenge for SBSP is the scale and continuous operating profile: managing gigawatt-class waste heat with lightweight radiators sized for the available solar illumination geometry at GEO, while meeting mass targets required for economic launch costs, has not been demonstrated. Radiator mass and area requirements for SBSP are significant and add to the satellite mass and structural complexity.

Feasibility and Claim Boundary

This page documents spacecraft thermal control technology as it applies to SBSP based on established thermal engineering principles and published SBSP system studies. It does not claim that thermal solutions for gigawatt-class SBSP satellites have been designed, built, or tested. Mass projections for SBSP thermal systems in conceptual studies carry significant uncertainty given the novel scale and continuous-operation requirements.
