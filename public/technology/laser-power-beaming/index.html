Laser Power Beaming for Space-Based Solar Power

Technology Reference

Laser power beaming is an alternative wireless energy transfer approach for SBSP that uses coherent laser light—typically in the near-infrared spectrum at wavelengths of 808 nm, 1064 nm, or other laser-efficient bands—to transmit power from an orbital satellite to a ground photovoltaic receiving station. Compared to microwave, laser beaming offers a much smaller beam divergence for a given transmitter aperture, enabling smaller receiving stations and potentially higher power density at the receiver. The ground receiver converts laser light directly to electricity using photovoltaic cells optimized for the laser wavelength, which can achieve high conversion efficiency. Key challenges include atmospheric absorption and scattering—particularly by clouds and aerosols—which can interrupt or attenuate the beam significantly, making laser power beaming weather-dependent in a way that microwave transmission largely avoids. High-power laser generation in space with sufficient wall-plug efficiency, thermal management of the laser source, and precise pointing over the geostationary distance are additional challenges. Laser beaming is considered more suitable for lower-power applications or orbital-to-orbital power transfer where atmospheric path is absent.

Infrastructure Role

Laser power beaming offers an alternative transmission pathway for SBSP that requires a different transmitter and receiver architecture than microwave. For small-scale or precision applications, it provides a compact, narrow-beam power transfer option. For terrestrial baseload power delivery, its weather sensitivity is a significant operational constraint that affects capacity factor and reliability.

Subsystem Relationship

The laser transmitter subsystem requires high-power, efficient laser sources and precise pointing systems on the satellite. Thermal management of the laser source is critical, as laser generation is less efficient than solid-state microwave amplifiers and produces more waste heat per unit output. Ground photovoltaic receivers optimized for the specific laser wavelength replace the microwave rectenna system.

Feasibility and Readiness

High-power laser systems are mature (TRL 9) in defense and industrial applications. Laser power beaming over terrestrial distances has been demonstrated at kilowatt levels (TRL 6–7). Space-to-ground laser beaming at SBSP power levels over geostationary distances has not been demonstrated. Weather-related availability limitations are well characterized and remain an unresolved operational challenge for continuous baseload power delivery.

Feasibility and Claim Boundary

This page describes laser power beaming as a studied SBSP transmission alternative based on published technical assessments and demonstrations. It does not claim that laser beaming has been demonstrated at SBSP power or distance scales, nor that weather availability can be addressed to meet baseload power requirements. Performance comparisons with microwave depend on system configuration and site weather, and are not resolved by current research.
