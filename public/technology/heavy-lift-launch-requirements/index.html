Heavy-Lift Launch Requirements for SBSP

Technology Reference

Heavy-lift launch requirements for space-based solar power reflect the fundamental constraint that SBSP satellites—consisting of very large masses of solar cells, structural elements, power electronics, and transmission systems—must be transported from Earth's surface to geostationary orbit, the location most suitable for continuous solar illumination and fixed terrestrial coverage. Proposed gigawatt-class SBSP systems require delivery of thousands to tens of thousands of metric tons of hardware to GEO. Current heavy-lift vehicles—including SpaceX Falcon Heavy, NASA SLS, and planned next-generation rockets—can deliver 20–70 metric tons to low Earth orbit (LEO), with substantially less to GEO. This creates a fundamental mismatch: a single GW-scale SBSP system would require hundreds to thousands of heavy-lift launches at current vehicle performance levels. Achieving economic viability requires either radical reduction of satellite specific mass (kg/kW), development of much larger launch vehicles, or in-space manufacturing that reduces the mass launched from Earth. Launch cost per kilogram to GEO—currently $5,000–$20,000/kg—is the dominant cost driver in most SBSP economic analyses.

Infrastructure Role

Launch capability is the primary bottleneck in the SBSP supply chain, determining how quickly the orbital infrastructure can be built and at what cost. The launch cost per kilogram to GEO, multiplied by the total system mass, is the largest single component of SBSP capital cost in most published economic analyses. Advances in reusable heavy-lift launch vehicles are considered a prerequisite for commercially viable SBSP.

Subsystem Relationship

Launch requirements are determined jointly by the satellite's structural design (mass) and the power output targets (specific mass in kg/kW). Modular spacecraft architecture and in-space assembly methods determine how the satellite mass is divided into individual launch payloads. In-space propulsion is needed to transfer hardware from LEO staging orbits to GEO following launch. Manufacturing systems that could produce components in orbit would reduce Earth-launch mass requirements.

Feasibility and Readiness

Current heavy-lift launch vehicles are at TRL 9. The launch capacity required to build GW-scale SBSP systems does not exist at any price point today. Next-generation vehicles such as SpaceX Starship aim to dramatically reduce cost-per-kg and increase payload to orbit, but have not yet demonstrated the reusability and reliability needed for the hundreds to thousands of launches an SBSP build-out would require. Cost projections are highly uncertain.

Feasibility and Claim Boundary

This page documents launch requirements for SBSP based on published system studies and current launch vehicle specifications. It does not claim specific future launch costs or that next-generation launch vehicles will achieve the performance improvements projected by their developers. Launch economics projections in SBSP studies use assumed cost-per-kg values that differ significantly between optimistic and conservative scenarios.
