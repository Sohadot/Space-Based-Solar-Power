Modular Spacecraft Architecture for SBSP

Technology Reference

Modular spacecraft architecture for space-based solar power refers to the design approach in which the SBSP satellite is conceived not as a single integrated structure but as an assembly of many standardized, interchangeable modules—each containing solar cells, power electronics, structural elements, and potentially transmission components—that can be mass-manufactured, launched separately, and assembled or aggregated in orbit. Modularity is considered essential for SBSP at gigawatt scale because it allows the satellite to be built incrementally, distributes manufacturing across standard production runs rather than requiring custom large-scale fabrication, enables in-orbit maintenance and replacement of failed units without decommissioning the entire satellite, and potentially allows satellites to be upgraded as technology improves. Key architectural decisions include the granularity of modularity (small 'sandwich' modules versus larger structural panels), the interface standards between modules (power, mechanical, data), and the degree to which modules are functionally independent versus requiring tight integration.

Infrastructure Role

Modular spacecraft architecture is the structural and systems engineering framework that makes SBSP construction and operation feasible at the required scale. It determines how the orbital platform interfaces with launch services, robotic assembly systems, and maintenance operations. The choice of modular architecture affects mass efficiency, manufacturing cost, assembly complexity, and operational reliability over the satellite's intended multi-decade service life.

Subsystem Relationship

Modular architecture is directly coupled to in-space assembly methods (which must handle and connect the modules), the launch and logistics subsystem (which delivers modules as payload), and robotic servicing systems (which perform module replacement). Power and structural interface standards between modules are critical design choices that affect system-level performance and serviceability.

Feasibility and Readiness

Modular spacecraft design principles are well-established (TRL 9 for conventional satellites). The specific challenge of designing modules that can be mass-manufactured at the extremely low specific mass required for SBSP, that maintain performance in GEO radiation and thermal environment over decades, and that can be assembled robotically into km-scale structures is at TRL 3–5. No modular SBSP architecture has been built or operated.

Feasibility and Claim Boundary

This page describes modular spacecraft architecture for SBSP based on published design studies and existing modular satellite concepts. It does not claim that any modular SBSP architecture has been validated in orbit or that mass and cost projections in conceptual studies reflect achievable engineering performance. Module interface standards for SBSP do not yet exist as formal specifications.
