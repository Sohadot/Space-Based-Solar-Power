Solar Array Structural Design for SBSP Satellites

Technology Reference

Solar array structural design for space-based solar power satellites addresses the engineering challenge of deploying, stabilizing, and operating photovoltaic collection structures of unprecedented scale in geostationary orbit. Proposed SBSP concepts require solar arrays ranging from several square kilometers to over 10 km² in area to generate gigawatt-class power levels. At this scale, traditional rigid panel approaches used on conventional satellites are impractical—structures must be ultra-lightweight, deployable or assemblea­ble in orbit, and capable of maintaining shape stability under solar radiation pressure, thermal gradients, and gravitational effects. Key design approaches include modular panel architectures that can be assembled robotically, deployable membrane structures, and truss-based frameworks. Structural dynamics—vibration modes, attitude disturbances caused by flexible structures, and thermal expansion—must be managed to maintain solar pointing accuracy and prevent fatigue failures over the multi-decade intended operational lifetime. Material choices involve composite materials, thin-film substrates, and novel lightweight structural members.

Infrastructure Role

The solar array structure is the physical backbone of the SBSP orbital collection subsystem. It provides mechanical support for photovoltaic cells, maintains array pointing toward the sun, and integrates with the power transmission subsystem and the overall satellite bus. Structural mass is a primary driver of launch cost; minimizing specific mass (kg/kW) of the array structure is a central design objective.

Subsystem Relationship

Structural design is tightly coupled to in-space assembly methods and robotic servicing systems, which must handle and join structural modules. Thermal management must account for differential thermal expansion across large structures. The attitude control subsystem must compensate for structural flexibility and solar radiation pressure torques. Power transmission alignment requirements impose pointing stability constraints on the structure.

Feasibility and Readiness

Ultra-lightweight deployable structures have been demonstrated at moderate scale on existing spacecraft (TRL 6–7). Large-scale in-orbit assembly of km²-class structures required for gigawatt SBSP has not been demonstrated and is assessed at TRL 2–4 for the SBSP application. Structural dynamics modeling at SBSP scale relies on extrapolation and simulation.

Feasibility and Claim Boundary

This page characterizes the structural design challenge for SBSP solar arrays based on published research and existing space structure technology. It does not claim that structures at the required SBSP scale have been built or tested. Mass and cost projections for SBSP structures cited in the literature carry significant uncertainty and should not be treated as established engineering specifications.
