On-Orbit Propulsion Systems for SBSP Satellites

Technology Reference

On-orbit propulsion systems for space-based solar power satellites provide the thrust and impulse needed for orbit raising from LEO staging orbits to GEO, station-keeping at GEO to counteract orbital perturbations, attitude control of the large satellite structure, and end-of-life disposal operations. Given the very large mass of SBSP satellites, propulsion requirements are substantial—orbit raising alone from LEO to GEO requires significant delta-V, and the propellant mass needed scales with satellite mass. High-efficiency electric propulsion systems—ion thrusters, Hall-effect thrusters—are strongly preferred over chemical propulsion for SBSP because their significantly higher specific impulse (Isp) reduces the propellant mass fraction for the orbit-raising and station-keeping maneuvers. Solar-powered electric propulsion is particularly attractive for SBSP because the solar arrays on the satellite can power the thrusters directly. The solar-electric orbit-raising trajectory from LEO to GEO is slow (months to years) compared to chemical propulsion (days), but substantially more mass-efficient. Continuous station-keeping throughout the satellite's operational lifetime requires reliable, long-life thrusters capable of sustained operation.

Infrastructure Role

On-orbit propulsion is essential for SBSP orbital operations: delivering hardware from launch-orbit staging locations to operational GEO slots, maintaining precise GEO station-keeping to ensure continuous ground coverage, and controlling the attitude of the large satellite structure. Propulsion system mass and propellant consumption affect the satellite's net power-to-mass ratio and lifetime mass budget.

Subsystem Relationship

Propulsion systems are powered by and mechanically integrated with the solar array and power conditioning subsystems. Station-keeping requirements interact with structural dynamics—thrust must be applied without exciting structural resonances. In-space logistics planning must account for the slow orbit-raising transit times of electric propulsion systems. End-of-life propulsion for GEO disposal or deorbit must be designed into the satellite architecture from the start.

Feasibility and Readiness

High-efficiency electric propulsion (ion and Hall-effect thrusters) is at TRL 9 for commercial GEO satellites. Operating electric propulsion on SBSP-scale satellites—where thruster arrays must provide thrust proportional to much larger satellite mass—and achieving the operating lifetime required for multi-decade SBSP operation has not been demonstrated at SBSP scale. The interaction between very large solar arrays and electric thruster plumes is an open technical question.

Feasibility and Claim Boundary

This page documents on-orbit propulsion systems for SBSP based on current space propulsion technology and published SBSP system study requirements. It does not claim that propulsion systems at SBSP scale have been demonstrated or that the propellant mass budgets for SBSP orbit-raising are fully optimized. Long-duration thruster life and plume-structure interaction effects specific to SBSP-scale platforms require further investigation.
