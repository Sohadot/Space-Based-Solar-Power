Robotic On-Orbit Servicing for SBSP

Technology Reference

Robotic on-orbit servicing for space-based solar power satellites encompasses the robotic systems, autonomous operations, and logistics infrastructure required to inspect, maintain, repair, and upgrade the orbital platform throughout its multi-decade service life without the involvement of human crew members in the vicinity of the satellite. Given the scale and complexity of SBSP satellites—potentially thousands of square kilometers of solar arrays and millions of individual modules—human servicing is neither practical nor economical. Robotic servicing systems must be capable of navigating the large structure, identifying failed or degraded modules, removing and replacing them with fresh units, and verifying successful replacement, all autonomously or under remote supervision from Earth with communication latency of up to 0.24 seconds at GEO. Key technology components include free-flying servicing robots or crawling robots that traverse the satellite structure, end-effectors capable of handling SBSP module connectors in the thermal and radiation environment of GEO, machine vision systems for inspection and guidance, and depot infrastructure for storing spare modules at the satellite.

Infrastructure Role

Robotic on-orbit servicing is an enabling subsystem for the long-term viability and economic life of SBSP satellites. Without servicing capability, degradation of individual modules over the satellite's lifetime would progressively reduce power output, and manufacturing defects or micrometeorite damage would cause unrecoverable failures. Effective servicing extends operational lifetime, improves reliability, and allows technology upgrades that extend economic value.

Subsystem Relationship

Robotic servicing systems interact with the modular spacecraft architecture (which must be designed for robotic handleability), the launch and logistics subsystem (which delivers replacement modules and servicing robots), and the autonomous fault detection subsystem (which identifies components requiring service). Power and data interfaces on the satellite must support the docking and operation of servicing vehicles.

Feasibility and Readiness

Robotic on-orbit servicing demonstrations have been conducted by DARPA (RSGS), NASA (OSAM), and commercial operators at LEO and GEO (Northrop Grumman MEV for GEO satellite life extension, TRL 7–8). Servicing the scale and module density of an SBSP satellite autonomously, without human supervision in the proximity, at the reliability and throughput required is at TRL 3–4. No SBSP-representative robotic servicing demonstration exists.

Feasibility and Claim Boundary

This page documents robotic on-orbit servicing technology for SBSP based on published literature and existing OOS demonstrations. It does not claim that autonomous robotic servicing of SBSP-scale structures has been demonstrated or that the autonomy and reliability levels required for uncrewed SBSP satellite servicing can be achieved with current robotic technology. Existing OOS demonstrations have involved far simpler structures than a full-scale SBSP satellite.
