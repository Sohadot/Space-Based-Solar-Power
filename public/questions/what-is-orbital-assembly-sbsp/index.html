What is orbital assembly and why does SBSP require it?

Reference Question

Orbital assembly refers to the construction or integration of large structures in space from components that are launched separately, then assembled in orbit using robotic systems, autonomous processes, or eventually human workers. Space-based solar power requires orbital assembly because a functional solar power satellite at the scale needed to deliver meaningful amounts of power would be far too large to launch as a single unit on any existing or near-term launch vehicle. A GEO satellite designed to deliver several gigawatts of power would have a solar collection area measured in square kilometers and would weigh thousands of tons, far beyond any single launch capacity. Components must be launched separately and assembled in orbit. Orbital assembly is one of the less-developed capabilities in the SBSP chain. The International Space Station is a historical example of modular orbital construction, but SBSP would require much larger scale, likely at higher orbit, and would benefit from robotic automation to reduce cost. No large-scale autonomous orbital assembly capability exists as of 2026.

Answer Boundary

Orbital assembly requirement is established from physics and engineering analysis. No large-scale GEO orbital assembly capability exists. Capability timeline claims require institutional source citation.
