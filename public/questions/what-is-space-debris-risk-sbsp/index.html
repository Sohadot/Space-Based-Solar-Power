What is the space debris risk for SBSP systems?

Reference Question

Space debris risk for SBSP refers to the potential for SBSP satellites to be damaged by existing orbital debris, and for SBSP operations to contribute to the debris environment. Geostationary orbit — the intended altitude for most SBSP designs — has a relatively lower debris density than low Earth orbit, but the large physical size of an SBSP satellite increases the collision cross-section. A debris impact on an SBSP solar array or transmitter could degrade performance, create new debris fragments, or, in extreme cases, disable the satellite. An SBSP constellation would itself add to the orbital population, requiring end-of-life disposal plans consistent with international guidelines. The large area of SBSP solar arrays also makes them vulnerable to micrometeorite erosion over time, which affects long-term power output. Active debris removal technologies and orbital traffic management are evolving areas of space governance that would need to mature alongside SBSP development. Space debris risk is treated as a governance constraint in SBSP feasibility assessments.

Answer Boundary

Space debris risk analysis for SBSP is based on published orbital environment models and existing debris tracking data. The specific debris risk profile for an operational SBSP satellite has not been empirically measured — no such satellite exists.
