How large would the solar array of a commercial SBSP satellite be?

Reference Question

Reference SBSP designs for a single gigawatt-class power satellite typically call for solar arrays spanning several square kilometres in total area. The 1979 NASA reference study described a 5-gigawatt SPS with solar arrays of roughly 50 square kilometres — a structure larger than most cities. Modern designs with higher-efficiency solar cells and improved power electronics reduce this figure, but even optimistic designs for a 2-GW system require arrays of multiple square kilometres. To compare: the International Space Station solar arrays span approximately 2,500 square metres — about 0.0025 square kilometres — and represent decades of incremental orbital construction. A commercial SBSP solar array would be hundreds to thousands of times larger than the ISS solar array, requiring a fundamentally different approach to orbital construction, likely involving autonomous robotic assembly of modular units. The physical size drives the mass-to-orbit requirement, which is the primary economic constraint on SBSP.

Answer Boundary

Array size estimates are derived from reference design studies (NASA 1979, ESA, JAXA). No SBSP solar array of any significant size has been constructed in orbit. ISS solar array data is from public NASA records.
