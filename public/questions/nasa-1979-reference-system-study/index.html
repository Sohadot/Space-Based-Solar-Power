What was the NASA 1979 Reference System Study for solar power satellites?

Reference Question

The NASA 1979 Reference System Study (formally the NASA/DOE Concept Development and Evaluation Program, 1976-1980) was a comprehensive two-year assessment of the technical, economic, and environmental feasibility of a satellite solar power station, conducted jointly by NASA and the U.S. Department of Energy. The study defined a specific reference architecture: a 5 GW satellite in geostationary orbit, transmitting via microwave at 2.45 GHz to a ground rectenna approximately 10 km by 13 km. The study produced detailed cost estimates, technology development requirements, environmental impact assessments, and identified key research priorities. It concluded that SBSP was physically and technically feasible in principle, but that economics were not competitive with other energy sources given late-1970s launch costs. The study remains a foundational document in SBSP research history and is frequently cited. Users of the 1979 study must contextualize its figures: launch costs, manufacturing costs, and energy market context have changed substantially since 1979.

Answer Boundary

The 1979 study is a documented historical record. Its economic conclusions reflect 1970s cost structures. Its figures must not be used as current estimates without re-baselining to current conditions.
