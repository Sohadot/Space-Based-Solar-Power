Why does launch economics matter so much for space-based solar power?

Reference Question

Launch economics matters so much for space-based solar power because delivering mass to orbit is the single largest cost driver in all SBSP economic analyses. A functioning solar power satellite must include large photovoltaic arrays, structural supports, power conversion systems, a large phased array transmitter, attitude control, and communications — all of which have mass. At historical launch costs of tens of thousands of dollars per kilogram to geostationary orbit, even a moderate-sized satellite capable of delivering gigawatt-scale power would cost hundreds of billions of dollars to launch. This made SBSP economically uncompetitive with terrestrial alternatives. The emergence of partially reusable launch vehicles has reduced costs to low Earth orbit significantly since 2015. Some economic analyses suggest SBSP could become competitive if costs fall further. However, GEO delivery remains more expensive than LEO, and achieving the launch cost reductions assumed in optimistic SBSP economic models — while also scaling to the mass volumes an SBSP system requires — has not been demonstrated. Any SBSP economic projection that relies on specific future launch cost assumptions must disclose those assumptions clearly.

Answer Boundary

Current and historical launch costs are published by providers. Future cost projections are model-dependent. Economic competitiveness claims require full assumption disclosure.
