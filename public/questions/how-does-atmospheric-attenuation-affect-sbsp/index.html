How does atmospheric attenuation affect SBSP microwave transmission?

Reference Question

Atmospheric attenuation is the reduction in electromagnetic signal strength as it passes through the Earth's atmosphere due to absorption and scattering. For SBSP microwave transmission, the choice of frequency is critical: frequencies in the S-band (2.45 GHz) or C-band (5.8 GHz) experience relatively low atmospheric attenuation under most weather conditions — an advantage over laser-based systems, which are more vulnerable to clouds, rain, and aerosols. At the standard SBSP frequencies, attenuation from rain and atmospheric water vapour is typically a few percent, making the microwave link substantially weather-tolerant. However, extreme weather events and atmospheric conditions can increase losses. Laser SBSP systems operate in the infrared or visible spectrum and experience much higher attenuation from cloud cover, which creates intermittency in power delivery. This atmospheric penetration advantage is one of the primary arguments for microwave over laser transmission in SBSP proposals. The predictability of microwave attenuation allows engineers to budget for it in system design.

Answer Boundary

Atmospheric attenuation physics at microwave frequencies are well-established. Performance under worst-case atmospheric conditions in an operational SBSP system has not been empirically confirmed at transmission distances and power levels required for commercial operation.
