What is specific mass in SBSP engineering analysis?

Reference Question

Specific mass in SBSP engineering analysis is the ratio of system mass to power output, typically expressed in kilograms per kilowatt (kg/kW). It is a normalised measure of how efficiently the SBSP system converts mass launched to orbit into delivered electrical power. Lower specific mass means more power per kilogram of launched hardware — which is desirable because it reduces the total mass-to-orbit requirement and therefore the launch cost for a given power output. The 1979 NASA reference study used specific mass figures that corresponded to thousands of tonnes per gigawatt. Modern SBSP proposals target specific masses of a few kg/kW or less. Achieving very low specific mass requires extremely lightweight structural materials, high-efficiency solar cells, and high-performance power electronics. In-space manufacturing from lightweight materials, or from materials sourced off-Earth, could further reduce specific mass. Specific mass is a key figure of merit for comparing SBSP designs and for assessing whether a design can approach economic viability.

Answer Boundary

Specific mass figures in SBSP analysis are derived from design studies and component-level performance data. No SBSP system has been built, so specific mass is a design parameter, not a measured operational value.
