What is the energy conversion chain in an SBSP system?

Reference Question

The SBSP energy conversion chain begins with solar irradiance — the electromagnetic radiation arriving from the sun in geostationary orbit at approximately 1,360 watts per square metre. Photovoltaic panels on the satellite convert this solar radiation into direct current electricity. That electricity powers a direct current-to-radio frequency converter, typically solid-state devices called magnetrons or transistor-based amplifiers, which generate microwave radiation at the transmit frequency (often 2.45 GHz or 5.8 GHz). The phased-array transmitter antenna then directs this microwave beam toward the Earth-side rectenna. At the rectenna, the received microwave energy is converted back to direct current by rectifying diodes embedded in the antenna elements. The output direct current is then conditioned — filtered, regulated, and converted to alternating current — for grid injection. Each conversion step introduces losses. The overall DC-to-DC efficiency from solar panel output to grid injection has been analysed in research contexts but has not been demonstrated end-to-end at commercial scale.

Answer Boundary

The conversion chain physics are established in engineering literature. End-to-end efficiency figures for the full chain at grid-relevant scale are derived from component-level analyses and modelling; no commercial SBSP system has demonstrated the full chain.
