Why is geostationary orbit preferred for space-based solar power?

Reference Question

Geostationary orbit (GEO) at approximately 35,786 km altitude is preferred for most SBSP designs because of two key properties. First, a satellite in GEO remains stationary above a fixed point on Earth's equator as seen from the ground. This means a fixed ground receiving station can continuously receive power from the satellite without tracking, greatly simplifying the receiving infrastructure. Second, at GEO altitude, the satellite spends approximately 99% of the year in direct sunlight, with brief eclipse periods only near the equinoxes. This near-continuous solar access is one of SBSP's primary advantages over terrestrial solar. The main disadvantage of GEO is the high cost and difficulty of launching mass to that altitude. GEO is substantially more expensive to reach per kilogram than low Earth orbit. This makes launch economics the dominant cost constraint for GEO-based SBSP designs. Some SBSP proposals use LEO constellations to reduce launch costs, but LEO introduces intermittency because individual satellites pass over a given location for only minutes at a time.

Answer Boundary

Geostationary orbital mechanics are established physics. Economic implications for SBSP are analytical and depend on launch cost assumptions that must be cited.
