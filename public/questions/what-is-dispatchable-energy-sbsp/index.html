What is dispatchable energy and can SBSP deliver it?

Reference Question

Dispatchable energy refers to electricity generation that can be turned on, turned off, or varied in output on demand by a grid operator. It contrasts with non-dispatchable sources like wind and solar, whose output depends on weather conditions and cannot be increased beyond what the resource allows. SBSP is theoretically dispatchable in two senses: first, because the beam can be reduced, steered away from the rectenna, or interrupted by the satellite; and second, because multiple SBSP satellites could be directed at different ground receivers to match shifting grid demand. This dispatchability is a significant advantage over terrestrial variable renewables and, if demonstrated, would make SBSP a premium resource in electricity markets that pay for reliability and controllability. The ability to direct a beam to different locations on Earth — potentially different countries or regions — also implies a form of global energy flexibility not available from fixed terrestrial infrastructure. Dispatchable SBSP delivery has not been demonstrated.

Answer Boundary

SBSP's theoretical dispatchability follows from the controllability of the microwave beam. No SBSP dispatchable power delivery has been demonstrated. The market value of dispatchable space power in actual electricity markets has not been established.
