Why was SBSP not built in the 1970s and 1980s despite early studies?

Reference Question

The landmark 1979 NASA reference study concluded that SBSP was technically feasible but economically impractical under the conditions of that era. The primary barrier was launch economics: placing the required mass in geostationary orbit using 1970s-era launch vehicles would have cost far more than competing energy alternatives. The study estimated that a full-scale SBSP system would require hundreds of Shuttle-equivalent launches per gigawatt of capacity — a cost that could not approach grid parity. Additionally, the political and budget environment shifted: declining oil prices in the early 1980s reduced the urgency of alternative energy investment, and the space industry did not prioritise large-scale orbital infrastructure. The Department of Energy, which co-sponsored the 1979 study, did not advance the programme to development. The concept was placed into research archives rather than eliminated, and it has been periodically revisited — by the European Space Agency, JAXA, and academic groups — as launch costs and technology have evolved.

Answer Boundary

The history of 1970s SBSP studies is documented in the 1979 NASA reference study and related DOE reports. The economic conclusions were specific to that era's launch costs. Whether current conditions make SBSP economically viable remains under study and has not been commercially confirmed.
