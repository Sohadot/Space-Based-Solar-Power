How does space-based solar power compare to terrestrial solar?

Reference Question

Space-based solar power and terrestrial solar differ across several important dimensions. Solar availability: A GEO satellite receives sunlight approximately 99% of the year at consistent intensity (about 1,361 W/m²), while terrestrial solar is limited by day-night cycles, seasons, and weather. This means SBSP could theoretically deliver continuous baseline power, while terrestrial solar produces variable output requiring storage or backup capacity. Land use: A large rectenna farm requires substantial land area, but the satellite collects energy from a much larger effective solar collection area per unit of ground footprint than a terrestrial farm at the same latitude. System complexity: Terrestrial solar is a commercially mature, low-complexity technology with established manufacturing and supply chains. SBSP requires technologies that have not been demonstrated at scale: orbital assembly, high-power wireless transmission, and large rectenna infrastructure. Cost: Terrestrial solar has declined dramatically in cost and is commercially competitive in many markets. SBSP economics remain unproven and depend heavily on future launch cost assumptions. The comparison is not a competition between equals: terrestrial solar is a mature commercial technology, SBSP is a long-horizon concept.

Answer Boundary

Physical comparisons are based on established parameters. Economic and performance comparisons require assumption-disclosure. Terrestrial solar maturity is documented; SBSP commercial readiness is not.
