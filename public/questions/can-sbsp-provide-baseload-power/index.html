Can SBSP provide baseload power to the electricity grid?

Reference Question

Baseload power refers to the minimum level of electrical demand that a grid must meet continuously, 24 hours a day. SBSP is theoretically capable of providing baseload power because a satellite in geostationary orbit receives sunlight approximately 99% of the time — it is only in Earth's shadow for brief periods around the equinoxes. Unlike terrestrial solar, SBSP is not limited by nighttime or weather. This continuous availability is one of the principal strategic advantages of SBSP over terrestrial renewables, which require storage or complementary generation to provide baseload. If an SBSP system can deliver power continuously at a stable output level, it can function as a baseload source. However, this capability depends on consistent beam control, mechanical reliability of the orbital platform, and ground reception system availability. No SBSP system has demonstrated continuous power delivery to a grid. The baseload characteristic of SBSP is a theoretical design property, not a demonstrated operational one.

Answer Boundary

SBSP's theoretical baseload capability is derived from the orbital geometry — continuous solar access in GEO. Whether SBSP can deliver reliable baseload at grid scale has not been demonstrated. Operational reliability figures do not exist for a non-existent system.
