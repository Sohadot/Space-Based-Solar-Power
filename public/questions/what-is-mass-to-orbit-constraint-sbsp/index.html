What is mass-to-orbit and why is it the primary constraint for SBSP?

Reference Question

Mass-to-orbit refers to the total mass of material that must be transported from Earth's surface to orbital altitude to build and operate an SBSP system. It is widely considered the primary economic constraint on SBSP because launch cost dominates the total system cost at current and near-term launch prices. A commercial SBSP system for 1 GW of delivered power would require placing thousands of tonnes of hardware in geostationary orbit — estimates vary by design but commonly range from 5,000 to 50,000 tonnes depending on efficiency assumptions. At even $2,000 per kilogram to GEO (lower than current prices), this implies $10 billion to $100 billion in launch costs per gigawatt. Reducing specific mass — the kilograms required per kilowatt of delivered power — and reducing launch cost per kilogram are the two independent levers that could make SBSP economically viable. Heavy-lift reusable launch vehicles are improving launch economics, but the required cost reductions for competitive SBSP are still being studied.

Answer Boundary

Mass-to-orbit requirements are derived from engineering analyses and reference design studies. No SBSP system has been built, so mass estimates are theoretical. Launch cost projections depend on market conditions and are subject to change.
