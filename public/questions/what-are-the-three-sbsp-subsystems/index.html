What are the three main subsystems of an SBSP system?

Reference Question

An SBSP system is conventionally analysed as three linked subsystems: the space segment, the transmission segment, and the ground segment. The space segment consists of large photovoltaic or concentrating solar arrays mounted on an orbital platform, usually envisioned in geostationary orbit. These arrays convert sunlight into electricity, which is then converted into microwave or laser radiation. The transmission segment is the wireless power link: a phased-array transmitter on the satellite directs a focused beam toward the terrestrial receiver. Beam steering and control systems keep the beam accurately aimed despite orbital motion and atmospheric variation. The ground segment is a rectenna array — a field of rectifying antennas that convert the incoming microwave radiation back into direct current electricity. That electricity is then conditioned and fed into the local grid. The efficiency and scale of each subsystem independently determines the overall system performance. No commercial SBSP system has integrated all three at power-grid-relevant scales.

Answer Boundary

The three-subsystem architecture is established in the SBSP engineering literature (NASA 1979, ESA studies, JAXA studies). Commercial integration of all three subsystems at grid-relevant scale has not been demonstrated.
