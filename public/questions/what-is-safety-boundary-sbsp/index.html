What is a safety boundary in SBSP power beaming?

Reference Question

A safety boundary in SBSP power beaming is the defined perimeter around the rectenna site beyond which the microwave power density must remain below established safety thresholds. Inside the rectenna field, power densities may be at or above the designed operational level. At the safety boundary, power density must fall below the limit set by regulatory standards for general population exposure — typically a small fraction of the operational intensity. SBSP designs achieve this through the natural Gaussian profile of the beam: intensity decreases rapidly away from the beam centre, so at a finite distance from the edge of the rectenna, intensity drops to negligible levels. The precise definition of the safety boundary depends on the beam shape, total transmitted power, and the applicable regulatory standard. Maintaining the safety boundary requires that the beam stays accurately aimed at the rectenna — if the beam wanders due to control failure, the safety boundary shifts with it, which is why beam control and automatic shut-off are considered critical safety systems in SBSP design.

Answer Boundary

Safety boundary concepts are defined in SBSP design studies and exist in the broader context of radio frequency safety standards. No operational SBSP system has defined, implemented, or measured a real-world safety boundary.
