What is power conversion efficiency in SBSP and why does it matter?

Reference Question

Power conversion efficiency in SBSP refers to the fraction of input energy that is delivered as useful output energy at each stage of the conversion chain. It matters because losses compound across multiple stages: solar-to-electric efficiency of the photovoltaic cells, electric-to-microwave efficiency of the transmitter, free-space transmission loss (which is predictable and geometric), atmospheric absorption, and microwave-to-electric efficiency of the rectenna diodes. Each percentage point of efficiency improvement at any stage reduces the size of hardware required to deliver the same power — which directly reduces mass-to-orbit requirements, cost, and structural complexity. Rectenna diode efficiency for microwave-to-DC conversion has been demonstrated at high levels in laboratory conditions (above 80% at specific frequencies). Solar cell efficiency improvements and solid-state power amplifier advances also drive the overall chain. The total end-to-end system efficiency — from sunlight incident on the orbital panel to electricity delivered to the grid — is the figure that determines commercial viability, and no commercial system has demonstrated this end-to-end.

Answer Boundary

Component-level efficiencies are documented in the research literature. End-to-end system efficiency at SBSP-relevant scales has been modelled but not demonstrated in a deployed commercial system.
