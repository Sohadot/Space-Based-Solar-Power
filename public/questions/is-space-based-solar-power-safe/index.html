Is space-based solar power safe? What are the safety considerations?

Reference Question

The primary safety question for space-based solar power involves the microwave power beam: at what power density does it pose a risk to humans, aircraft, or ecosystems? International guidelines exist for microwave exposure from organizations including IEEE and ICNIRP. SBSP designs generally assume that the power density at the center of the rectenna, and within any exclusion zone, will remain within these guidelines. Outside exclusion zones, the power density is designed to fall below exposure limits. Aircraft safety corridors would need to be established around the beam path. Additional considerations include radiofrequency interference with existing communications systems, orbital debris generated by large satellite structures, and the environmental impact of large ground receiving stations. At the power densities discussed in most SBSP designs, birds and wildlife within the exclusion zone — which is not a hard-beam kill zone — would not be exposed to harmful levels. However, SBSP-specific safety regulations and licensing frameworks do not yet exist. Safety verification would require design-specific analysis and regulatory coordination before any deployment.

Answer Boundary

General microwave exposure guidelines are established. SBSP-specific safety regulations do not exist. Claims about safety for specific designs require engineering analysis and regulatory review.
