How does beam steering work in SBSP power transmission?

Reference Question

Beam steering in SBSP refers to the ability to direct the microwave or laser transmission beam precisely at the ground receiver and adjust its aim continuously. In a phased array system, beam steering is achieved electronically: by varying the phase of the signal fed to each antenna element, the direction of peak constructive interference — and thus the beam direction — is shifted without moving any physical structure. This is called electronic steering or beam control. A pilot signal sent from the ground receiver back to the satellite can be used as a reference to keep the beam locked on target — a technique called retrodirective steering. In retrodirective arrays, the satellite transmitter automatically aims toward the source of the pilot signal, providing inherent safety: if the pilot signal is lost, the beam disperses rather than staying fixed. Laser-based SBSP systems use optical steering mechanisms, which are mechanically and optically different but serve the same purpose of maintaining beam-on-target alignment. Beam control accuracy requirements for SBSP are stringent: the beam must stay within a defined target area to avoid irradiating unintended zones.

Answer Boundary

Retrodirective phased arrays and beam steering principles are established in the literature and have been tested at laboratory and small demonstration scale. Full SBSP beam control at operational distances and power levels has not been demonstrated.
