What is defense energy resilience and how does SBSP relate to it?

Reference Question

Defence energy resilience refers to the ability of military forces to maintain power supply under conditions of conflict, disruption, or infrastructure attack. A military that depends on fixed terrestrial grid infrastructure for power is vulnerable when that infrastructure is attacked or damaged. SBSP relates to defence energy resilience because it could provide a power supply that is independent of terrestrial grid infrastructure: a satellite in orbit, delivering power via a directed beam to mobile or remote receivers, cannot be interdicted by damaging ground-based power lines or generating stations. This concept has been studied by defence organisations in several countries. The UK Ministry of Defence and US Department of Defense have both considered SBSP in the context of energy security and resilience. A satellite in GEO is not invulnerable — it can be targeted by anti-satellite weapons — but it provides a different threat surface than terrestrial infrastructure. SBSP for defence is more mature as a strategic concept than as a deployed capability.

Answer Boundary

Defence energy resilience as a strategic concept for SBSP is documented in institutional assessments. No defence SBSP system has been deployed. Anti-satellite threat analysis is a separate body of work. All claims about defence SBSP are at the strategic concept and study level.
