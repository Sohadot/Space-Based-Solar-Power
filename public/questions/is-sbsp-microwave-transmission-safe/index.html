Is SBSP microwave transmission safe for people and wildlife?

Reference Question

SBSP microwave transmission safety depends on the power density of the beam at ground level. SBSP designs typically propose central beam intensities of 23 milliwatts per square centimetre — approximately equal to the intensity of sunlight at Earth's surface — with much lower intensities at the edges of the rectenna field. At these intensities, the microwave energy is comparable to or below levels that regulatory bodies have established as safe for occupational and general-population exposure. The design principle of SBSP is that beam intensity outside the rectenna perimeter would be well below any established safety threshold. The rectenna area itself would be managed as an exclusion zone or dual-use agricultural area (since most of the beam passes through the rectenna elements, the land beneath can be used for farming). Studies of biological effects of microwave radiation at the proposed SBSP power densities have not identified clear health hazards, but critics note that long-term continuous low-level exposure is not fully characterised. Safety boundaries — keeping the beam properly aimed at the rectenna — are enforced by the beam control system.

Answer Boundary

SBSP safety analysis is based on proposed designs and existing microwave exposure research. No operational SBSP system exists, so no direct empirical safety data from an operational system is available. Biological effects of long-term low-level microwave exposure remain an active area of research.
