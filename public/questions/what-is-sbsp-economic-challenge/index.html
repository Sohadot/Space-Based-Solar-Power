What is the economic challenge facing commercial SBSP development?

Reference Question

The economic challenge for commercial SBSP is that the cost of delivering power to the grid must compete with established alternatives — including terrestrial solar, wind, nuclear, and fossil generation — on a per-kilowatt-hour basis. At current launch costs, the capital investment to place an SBSP system in orbit is orders of magnitude higher than the capital investment for equivalent terrestrial solar capacity. Falling launch costs from reusable launch vehicles are improving this picture, but analysts differ on how much further cost reduction is required. SBSP also has advantages that are difficult to monetise in standard electricity markets: 24-hour availability without storage, beamable delivery to any location on Earth, and independence from weather. These characteristics may have value in military, remote, or disaster-response contexts that justify higher per-kWh cost. The SBSP economic analysis is complicated by the lack of demonstrated end-to-end systems, which means cost estimates carry large uncertainty ranges. Independent institutional assessments (ESA, JAXA, UK Space Energy Initiative) have concluded that SBSP may become economically competitive as launch costs fall but have not declared it currently viable.

Answer Boundary

Economic comparisons are based on modelling and institutional assessments, not deployed systems. Cost projections depend on future launch prices, technology performance, and energy market conditions — all of which carry uncertainty.
