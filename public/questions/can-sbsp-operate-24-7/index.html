Can space-based solar power operate 24 hours a day, 7 days a week?

Reference Question

A solar power satellite in geostationary orbit can receive sunlight approximately 99% of the year, making near-continuous operation theoretically achievable. The brief interruptions occur during eclipse periods around the spring and autumn equinoxes when Earth's shadow crosses the satellite's orbital plane. These eclipses last at most about 72 minutes per day during the equinox period, which spans a few weeks per year. For the remaining roughly 99% of the year, no eclipse occurs. This near-continuous availability is one of the defining advantages of GEO-based SBSP over terrestrial solar: there is no night-side outage and no weather-related intermittency. However, ground reception is still subject to the geometry of the beam and the fixed receiving station location. A rectenna in the northern hemisphere receives the beam at an angle, not perpendicular, which affects collection efficiency. Furthermore, near-continuous operation of the satellite does not address terrestrial cloud cover, though microwave beams penetrate clouds with relatively low attenuation.

Answer Boundary

GEO eclipse geometry is established orbital mechanics. Atmospheric transmission efficiency is frequency-dependent and well characterized. Operational continuity claims for specific designs require full system context.
