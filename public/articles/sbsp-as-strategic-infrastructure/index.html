SBSP as Strategic Energy Infrastructure: Beyond Conventional Power Generation

Infrastructure Strategy

Space-based solar power is not primarily a power generation technology in the conventional sense — it is a proposed infrastructure category that would establish a new relationship between orbital systems and terrestrial energy grids. This brief examines why the strategic framing of SBSP as infrastructure matters for how feasibility is assessed, how development timelines are structured, and what institutional actors treat it as a long-term strategic asset rather than a near-term product. The infrastructure framing also explains why multiple space agencies and governments have chosen to study SBSP even while acknowledging that no deployment-ready system exists.

Strategic Thesis

SBSP is better understood as a category of strategic energy infrastructure — comparable in scale and dependency to major grid investment, satellite communication systems, or nuclear energy programmes — than as a conventional power generation product. This framing shapes what feasibility means, what timelines are credible, and what institutional actors have strategic reasons to invest in it.

Institutional Context

National space agencies and government-commissioned studies frame SBSP as a long-term infrastructure option rather than a near-term commercial product. ESA's SOLARIS assessment, the UK Frazer-Nash feasibility study, and JAXA's SSPS roadmap all treat SBSP within an infrastructure development paradigm — identifying technology development phases, regulatory groundwork, and international coordination as prerequisites rather than obstacles. This distinction between 'product readiness' and 'infrastructure development pathway' is central to understanding how programme timelines are set and why they extend across decades.

Technical Context

Infrastructure-scale SBSP requires solving not only individual technology challenges (wireless power transmission, solar cell efficiency, in-space assembly) but also system integration at a scale that has no direct precedent. A single SBSP satellite capable of delivering gigawatt-scale power would be among the largest structures ever assembled in orbit. The infrastructure classification also implies that development costs are not recouped through a single product sale but through long-term operational capacity — which changes the economic model compared to conventional satellite programmes.

Feasibility Boundary

No infrastructure-scale SBSP system has been built or fully costed. Feasibility assessments — including ESA SOLARIS and the Frazer-Nash study — conclude that SBSP is technically achievable but dependent on technology cost reductions (particularly launch costs and power electronics) that have not yet been realised at the required scale. Feasibility must be understood as conditional on a technology development pathway, not as an established fact.

Claim Boundary

This brief documents the institutional infrastructure framing of SBSP and its analytical implications. It does not claim that SBSP is commercially viable, that any deployment programme is funded, or that infrastructure-scale deployment is imminent. All programme timelines cited are planning assumptions from their source documents, not confirmed commitments.
