Energy Sovereignty and the Strategic Rationale for Space-Based Solar Power

Energy Sovereignty and AI Demand

Energy sovereignty — the capacity of a state to secure reliable, affordable energy without structural dependence on external suppliers — has re-emerged as a strategic priority following the energy market disruptions of 2021–2023. Space-based solar power is one of a small number of proposed energy technologies that could, in principle, provide import-independent baseload power at national scale. This brief examines how energy sovereignty frames the strategic case for SBSP and what it implies for how governments should evaluate SBSP investment.

Strategic Thesis

Energy sovereignty framing elevates SBSP from a niche technology option to a potential strategic infrastructure investment, because SBSP's primary strategic attribute — independence from terrestrial geography, weather, and fuel supply chains — aligns directly with sovereignty objectives. This does not make SBSP the right investment choice, but it changes the decision framework compared to evaluating SBSP as a simple economic option.

Institutional Context

The energy sovereignty rationale for SBSP is most explicit in European institutional communications. The REPowerEU agenda, motivated by dependence reduction following the 2022 energy crisis, provided political context in which ESA's SOLARIS initiative was framed. The UK Space Energy Initiative explicitly positions SBSP as contributing to UK energy security. The Indian national SBSP programme is motivated partly by India's ambition to reduce fossil fuel import dependence. These different geopolitical contexts produce different strategic weights for the same technology.

Technical Context

SBSP's energy sovereignty claim rests on its technical characteristics: solar energy collected in space is not subject to weather, season, or terrestrial geography, meaning that any country with a rectenna field and the ability to deploy or access an SBSP satellite could receive continuous power independent of local renewable resource endowment or fossil fuel access. This characteristic is genuine but conditional on system availability — which itself depends on the orbital maintenance, launch logistics, and satellite supply chain that would underpin SBSP infrastructure.

Feasibility Boundary

Energy sovereignty through SBSP is conditional on deployment at infrastructure scale, which requires technology development, cost reductions, regulatory frameworks, and institutional coordination not currently in place. The sovereignty argument is a motivating factor for investment in the development pathway, not a confirmation that the pathway will succeed.

Claim Boundary

This brief examines the energy sovereignty framing of SBSP and its implications for strategic investment evaluation. It does not claim that SBSP provides energy sovereignty today, that any country has achieved energy sovereignty through SBSP, or that sovereignty framing resolves the technology and economic challenges.
