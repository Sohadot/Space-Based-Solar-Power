Caltech SSPP and the MAPLE Experiment: What In-Space Demonstration Establishes

Institutional Programs

The 2023 Caltech MAPLE experiment demonstrated targeted wireless power transmission in space for the first time — transmitting power between two points on the SSPD-1 satellite using a phased array transmitter, and detecting power at a receiver. This was a significant technology milestone. This brief examines precisely what MAPLE established, what it did not establish, and why the distinction matters for understanding SBSP technology progress.

Strategic Thesis

MAPLE confirmed that integrated phased-array WPT in a space environment is physically achievable and technically operable — advancing the TRL of this subsystem meaningfully. It did not confirm transmission chain efficiency at SBSP-relevant power levels, space-to-ground transmission across operational distances, beam control at geostationary orbit separation, or system integration of solar collection and transmission. The experiment's significance is real but bounded.

Institutional Context

Caltech's Space Solar Power Project was founded by philanthropic investment and represents the most significant private-sector SBSP development effort by an academic institution. SSPD-1 (Space Solar Power Demonstrator 1), launched in January 2023, carried three experiments: DOLCE (structural deployment), ALBA (photovoltaic efficiency), and MAPLE (wireless power transmission). Caltech published peer-reviewed results from MAPLE in 2023. The project has received significant media attention but has been careful in its own communications to distinguish demonstration scope from deployment claims.

Technical Context

MAPLE demonstrated that a phased array transmitter integrated with a photovoltaic panel could receive solar power and transmit it wirelessly to two target receivers within the same satellite structure — approximately 30 centimetres apart. The demonstration confirmed that phase coherence across the transmitter array could be maintained in a space environment. The power levels demonstrated were milliwatts, not the megawatt-to-gigawatt range relevant to SBSP. Transmission distance was centimetres, not the 36,000 kilometres of a geostationary satellite to ground.

Feasibility Boundary

MAPLE demonstrated component-level WPT in space at milliwatt power levels and centimetre distances. The gap between this demonstration and SBSP-scale operation involves many orders of magnitude in power and distance, with no intermediate-scale demonstrations yet performed. Component demonstration does not extrapolate directly to system performance — additional demonstrations at intermediate scales are required to establish the scaling pathway.

Claim Boundary

This brief documents what MAPLE demonstrated and what it did not demonstrate. It does not claim that MAPLE confirms SBSP deployment viability or commercial readiness. No overclaim framing of MAPLE as 'proving SBSP works' is appropriate — it proved a specific component technology works in a space environment at small scale.
