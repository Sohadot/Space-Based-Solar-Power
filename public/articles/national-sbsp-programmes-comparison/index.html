National SBSP Programmes: Comparing Institutional Approaches Across Space Agencies

Institutional Programs

Space-based solar power is being studied by national space agencies and research institutions across multiple continents, but the approaches differ significantly in strategic framing, institutional structure, technology focus, and urgency. This brief compares how NASA, ESA, JAXA, and national programmes in the UK, China, India, and South Korea approach SBSP — and what these differences reveal about how each institution frames the technology's strategic relevance.

Strategic Thesis

The diversity of national SBSP approaches reflects different underlying strategic logics: ESA frames SBSP primarily as an energy security response; JAXA frames it as a long-term energy technology development programme; China frames it as a national strategic infrastructure ambition; UK and US defence-linked programmes frame it as an energy logistics solution. These framings are not interchangeable — they produce different research priorities, timelines, and institutional commitments.

Institutional Context

ESA's SOLARIS is the most structured European assessment. JAXA's SSPS programme has the longest institutional history, with research spanning over three decades. NASA's SBSP revisit is a relatively recent re-engagement after the agency moved away from SBSP following the 1979 programme. China's national SBSP programme is the most explicitly infrastructure-ambition-driven, with goals for test facilities and satellite demonstrations expressed in national plans. The UK programme, via the Space Energy Initiative, is industry-consortium driven with government commissioning of feasibility studies. US defence-linked programmes (AFRL, Northrop Grumman SSPIDR) frame SBSP as a military energy logistics capability.

Technical Context

Technical focus areas differ by programme. JAXA and Caltech focus heavily on WPT component development. ESA SOLARIS takes a system architecture and technology roadmap approach. China's programme has invested in large-scale ground test facilities for WPT. Northrop Grumman SSPIDR focuses specifically on phased array transmitter technology for defence applications. These different foci are partly driven by strategic framing and partly by existing institutional and industrial capabilities.

Feasibility Boundary

No national programme has progressed beyond technology development and feasibility assessment phases. All national roadmaps include contingencies dependent on technology progress, cost reductions, and policy decisions not yet made. Programme funding in most cases is at the research and assessment level, not the development and deployment level.

Claim Boundary

This brief compares national programme structures and framing based on publicly available programme documentation. It does not claim that any national programme has committed to deployment, that programme roadmap dates are funded or confirmed, or that different national approaches imply equivalent deployment probability.
