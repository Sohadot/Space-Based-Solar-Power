From Demonstration to Infrastructure: The SBSP Development Pathway

Infrastructure Strategy

Space-based solar power has existed as a concept since 1968 and as a structured institutional study since the NASA/DoE programme of the late 1970s. Yet no deployment has occurred. This brief examines what a credible development pathway from current technology demonstrations to infrastructure-scale deployment would require, why the gap between demonstration and deployment is larger for SBSP than for most technologies, and what conditions — technological, economic, regulatory, and institutional — would need to change for that pathway to become viable.

Strategic Thesis

The gap between current SBSP demonstration activities and infrastructure-scale deployment is not a simple function of cost reduction or political will — it is a multi-layer system integration challenge that spans technology readiness, launch economics, regulatory precedent, grid policy, and international coordination. Understanding the development pathway means mapping these interdependencies, not just tracking individual technology metrics.

Institutional Context

JAXA's SPS roadmap, ESA SOLARIS, NASA's SBSP revisit, and the UK study all propose multi-decade development pathways with staged technology demonstrations, incremental system scaling, and policy development running in parallel. These pathways share a common structure: component demonstration (current), subsystem integration demonstration (near-term planned), small-scale pilot (mid-term conditional), and infrastructure-scale deployment (long-term contingent). The contingencies increase at each stage.

Technical Context

Technology readiness across SBSP subsystems is uneven. Wireless power transmission has been demonstrated in space at small scale (Caltech MAPLE, 2023). High-efficiency solar cells for space are in ongoing development. In-space assembly at SBSP-relevant scales has not been demonstrated. Heavy-lift launch at the required cadence and cost does not currently exist. Each subsystem must reach readiness thresholds before system integration testing can begin — and system integration at SBSP scale introduces challenges beyond those of individual subsystem development.

Feasibility Boundary

No deployment pathway is funded or confirmed. All institutional roadmaps are planning documents that assume technology development, cost reduction, and policy development occurring on assumed timelines. The roadmaps are conditional, not commitments. The gap between 2023-era TRL status and deployment readiness remains large for multiple critical subsystems.

Claim Boundary

This brief documents how institutional studies frame the SBSP development pathway. It does not claim that deployment is imminent, that any specific timeline is credible without the stated conditions being met, or that current demonstration activities confirm deployment viability. All roadmap timelines cited are institutional planning assumptions.
