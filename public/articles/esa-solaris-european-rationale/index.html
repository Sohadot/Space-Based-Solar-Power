ESA SOLARIS: European Strategic Rationale and Assessment Context

Institutional Programs

ESA's SOLARIS initiative is the most significant institutional commitment to SBSP assessment by a major space agency in the current era. Launched through ESA's Concurrent Design Facility, SOLARIS assessed SBSP system options, technology requirements, cost envelopes, and strategic rationale from a European perspective. This brief examines what SOLARIS concluded, how it frames the European strategic case for SBSP investment, and what it explicitly did not resolve.

Strategic Thesis

SOLARIS represents a formal institutional assessment that SBSP merits continued study and targeted technology investment — not a commitment to deployment. The assessment's strategic significance lies in ESA formalising SBSP as a legitimate research priority within the European space agenda, making it eligible for structured funding and programme development rather than ad hoc research.

Institutional Context

ESA presented SOLARIS to its member states as a potential new initiative requiring dedicated funding. The initiative was structured around three phases: a concept assessment phase (completed), a technology development phase (proposed), and a demonstration phase (conditional on technology progress). The European context includes EU energy security policy, the REPowerEU agenda, and the political salience of energy import dependence following the 2022 energy crisis. Industry players including Airbus and Thales Alenia Space have engaged with SOLARIS as a potential future market.

Technical Context

SOLARIS assessed multiple system architectures for European SBSP, evaluating microwave transmission vs laser options, single-satellite vs constellation approaches, and European vs international launch options. The assessment identified wireless power transmission technology development as the primary near-term investment priority. The study set out specific efficiency and performance targets that would need to be met at demonstration phase before proceeding to pilot deployment.

Feasibility Boundary

SOLARIS concluded that SBSP is technically feasible in principle but requires substantial technology development before any deployment decision. The assessment did not confirm commercial viability, funded programme approval, or a committed deployment timeline. The transition from assessment to funded technology development programme depends on ESA member state approval and budget allocation that has not been confirmed.

Claim Boundary

This brief documents the SOLARIS assessment process, conclusions, and strategic framing. It does not claim that SOLARIS confirms SBSP deployment viability, that ESA has committed to a SBSP programme, or that European industry involvement implies deployment contracts. All assessments cited reflect the study's conclusions and assumptions.
