Launch Economics and SBSP Viability: The Cost Threshold Problem

Technology and Feasibility

Launch cost is the single economic variable most frequently cited as the primary barrier to space-based solar power deployment. This brief examines the relationship between launch cost, mass-to-orbit requirements, and system economics for SBSP — why the launch cost threshold is where it is, how dramatically it needs to change from historical baselines for SBSP economics to close, and what recent developments in launch markets mean for the long-term trajectory of this constraint.

Strategic Thesis

SBSP economics are fundamentally sensitive to launch cost because of the extreme mass that must be placed in geostationary orbit. Historical analyses set the required launch cost reduction at roughly one to two orders of magnitude from late-20th-century baselines. Reductions achieved by commercial launch providers since the 2010s have moved the threshold meaningfully but have not yet brought SBSP economics to closure for conventional business cases — the question is whether the trajectory of cost reduction continues.

Institutional Context

Every major SBSP feasibility study from the NASA 1979 Reference System Study through to the ESA SOLARIS assessment and the Frazer-Nash study treats launch cost as a primary economic variable. The Frazer-Nash study modelled SBSP cost scenarios under various launch cost assumptions, finding that SBSP becomes economically competitive only under aggressive cost-reduction assumptions. NASA's SBSP revisit and ESA SOLARIS both note the potential impact of commercial launch cost reductions but stop short of treating current commercial launch pricing as sufficient to close the SBSP business case.

Technical Context

SBSP satellites in geostationary orbit must be launched to approximately 36,000 km altitude. A commercially viable SBSP satellite would mass thousands of tonnes in orbit — two to three orders of magnitude heavier than conventional communication satellites. Even at current commercial launch prices (in the tens of millions of dollars per tonne to low Earth orbit), the launch cost component of SBSP capital expenditure would be enormous. Mass-to-orbit efficiency — how much usable power can be generated per kilogram in orbit — is a parallel design driver. Specific mass (W/kg) of the solar array and transmission system directly determines how much launch cost can be tolerated for a given target cost per kWh.

Feasibility Boundary

No SBSP system has been costed with confirmed launch contracts. All economic analyses use modelled launch cost assumptions. The required launch cost for SBSP commercial viability depends on system design assumptions that are themselves unconfirmed. Current commercial launch prices represent a significant improvement over historical baselines but have not been demonstrated to close the SBSP economic case under conventional electricity market assumptions.

Claim Boundary

This brief documents the relationship between launch economics and SBSP viability as established in institutional feasibility studies. It does not claim that launch costs have reached SBSP viability thresholds, that any specific launch cost makes SBSP commercially competitive, or that future cost reductions are assured. All figures cited are from their source feasibility studies with their stated assumptions.
