Baseload Power

Glossary Term

Continuous stable electrical power generation meeting minimum grid demand at all times, regardless of weather or time of day.

Baseload power refers to the continuous, stable generation capacity that operates to meet the minimum level of electricity demand on a grid at all times, including overnight and periods of low demand. Baseload power plants run at consistently high capacity factors and provide the foundation of grid stability. SBSP proponents argue that an orbital solar power system could provide baseload-compatible power, unlike terrestrial wind or solar which are intermittent. This is because a geostationary orbital platform receives near-continuous solar irradiance regardless of Earth's day-night cycle or weather patterns. Whether SBSP could function as true baseload depends on the transmission system's continuous operability, the beam control system's reliability, the rectenna field's availability, and the grid integration architecture.

Claim Boundary

Baseload power concepts are well-established in power systems engineering. SBSP's ability to provide true baseload has not been demonstrated in operational systems.
