Microwave Transmission Pathway

Glossary Term

The end-to-end microwave energy pathway from orbital transmitter through atmosphere to terrestrial rectenna.

The microwave transmission pathway in SBSP encompasses the complete chain of energy conversion, beam formation, atmospheric propagation, and reception for a microwave-based power delivery system. Starting from conversion of solar-generated DC power to microwave radiation aboard the orbital transmitter, through the phased-array transmitter aperture, across the atmospheric propagation path, to the rectenna array where microwave energy is reconverted to DC electricity, each step involves efficiency losses and technical constraints. Microwave frequencies in the 2.45 GHz or 5.8 GHz range are commonly studied for SBSP because they pass through cloud cover and precipitation with relatively low attenuation compared to optical frequencies. The microwave pathway requires precise beam control to maintain alignment with the terrestrial rectenna across orbital motion.

Claim Boundary

Microwave transmission pathways are well-understood in principle and demonstrated at sub-scale. Full orbital-to-ground microwave transmission for SBSP has not been demonstrated.
