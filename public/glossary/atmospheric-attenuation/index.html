Atmospheric Attenuation

Glossary Term

Reduction of beam energy intensity as electromagnetic radiation passes through the atmosphere due to absorption and scattering.

Atmospheric attenuation refers to the reduction in beam energy intensity that occurs when electromagnetic radiation passes through Earth's atmosphere due to absorption and scattering by atmospheric gases, water vapor, clouds, precipitation, and aerosols. For SBSP power beaming, the degree of attenuation depends on the transmission frequency or wavelength. Microwave frequencies typically used in SBSP proposals experience relatively low attenuation under most atmospheric conditions, allowing power transmission through cloud cover and moderate precipitation. Laser frequencies experience substantially higher attenuation, particularly under overcast or rainy conditions, which limits the operational availability of laser SBSP pathways. Atmospheric attenuation affects both total system efficiency and the geographic and meteorological conditions under which a receiving site can operate effectively.

Claim Boundary

Atmospheric attenuation of electromagnetic radiation is well-characterized in the scientific literature. Operational SBSP experience with atmospheric attenuation under system conditions does not exist.
