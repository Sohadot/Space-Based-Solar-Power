Mass to Orbit

Glossary Term

The mass of material that must be transported from Earth to orbit to construct and operate an SBSP system.

Mass to orbit is the quantity of material that must be lifted from Earth's surface to an orbital altitude to construct, supply, and maintain a space system. For SBSP, mass to orbit is the fundamental economic constraint because all solar panels, structural elements, transmitter arrays, power electronics, and station-keeping systems must either be launched from Earth or produced from space-sourced materials that do not yet exist at commercial scale. The cost of launch — historically measured in thousands to tens of thousands of dollars per kilogram — dominates SBSP cost estimates. Reductions in launch cost driven by reusable launch vehicles are frequently cited as a condition for SBSP economic viability, though the mass required for SBSP at commercial scale remains enormous even at significantly reduced per-kilogram costs.

Claim Boundary

Mass-to-orbit requirements are calculable from engineering analyses. Actual SBSP construction has not been undertaken; cost projections depend on future launch economics that have not been fully realized.
